Muscle heatmaps

A muscle heatmap is a visual estimate of which muscles your training emphasises, based on your logged exercises.

What it means

LiftShift maps your logged exercises to the muscles they primarily and secondarily target. It then visualises your relative weekly emphasis as a colour-coded body map. Darker/hotter regions indicate more training volume directed at that muscle group.

This is an estimate to help you see patterns, not a direct measurement of hypertrophy or EMG activation. It tells you where your training effort is going — and what you might be neglecting.

Rolling 7-day windows

Unlike basic app heatmaps that use fixed calendar weeks, LiftShift uses rolling 7-day windows. This matches how your body actually recovers — muscles don’t reset on Sunday. If you trained chest on Wednesday and again the following Tuesday, that’s two sessions in a 7-day window, even though they fell in different calendar weeks. The heatmap reflects real recovery overlap.

Volume zone scoring

Each muscle receives a volume zone based on your weekly set count, personalised to your training history:

  • Maintenance — Enough sets to hold current muscle size and strength. Typically a lower weekly set count.
  • Growth — Within the range where progressive overload typically drives muscle and strength adaptation.
  • Overreaching — Weekly sets above what you can likely recover from. Sustained overreaching can lead to stagnation or injury.

How to use the heatmap

  • Spot imbalances — Is one side consistently hotter? Is chest overloaded compared to back? These patterns can guide exercise selection.
  • Click any muscle to see exactly which exercises contribute, with primary and secondary involvement weighted separately. You might discover that your “lat” volume is coming mostly from secondary contributions in rows, not from direct lat work.
  • Check the “top 3 concentration” metric — A warning if too much of your total volume goes to just three muscle groups. Green = well-distributed. Red = you might be neglecting something important.
  • Use calendar filtering with the heatmap to compare how your muscle emphasis shifts between training blocks.

Limitations

  • Exercise naming affects mapping — “Bench Press” maps to chest/triceps/front delts as expected, but unusual exercise names may not map correctly.
  • Variation matters — A close-grip bench press targets triceps more than a wide-grip bench press, but both may map similarly unless LiftShift recognises the variation.
  • Not a direct measure — The heatmap shows training emphasis, not actual muscle growth. Genetics, nutrition, sleep, and intensity all affect outcomes.
  • Bodyweight exercises — Pull-ups, dips, and push-ups map to muscles but their training effect depends on bodyweight and rep quality, which the heatmap can’t fully account for.
