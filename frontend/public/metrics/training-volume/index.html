Training volume

Definition first, then interpretation.

What training volume means

In LiftShift, “volume” refers to the amount of work you performed, derived from your logged sets. The standard representation is weight × reps summed across all sets within a workout, week, or any selected date range.

For example: if you did 3 sets of squats — 100 kg x 8 reps, 100 kg x 7 reps, and 100 kg x 6 reps — your squat volume for that workout is (100 × 8) + (100 × 7) + (100 × 6) = 2,100 kg.

What changes volume

  • More sets — Adding extra sets to an exercise increases total volume.
  • More reps per set — Going from 8 to 10 reps at the same weight ups your volume.
  • Heavier load — Even keeping sets and reps the same, adding 5 kg to the bar raises volume.
  • More exercises — Adding accessory lifts increases total session volume.

How to interpret volume trends

  • Rising trend — Usually indicates higher training stress, assuming similar exercise selection. If you’re recovering well and performance is improving, rising volume is a good sign.
  • Large spikes — Can be caused by one unusually long session, a new exercise, or a change in training block. Compare with your consistency chart to check if it was planned or accidental.
  • Volume plateaus — If volume stays flat for weeks while weight or reps aren’t increasing, you may have stalled. LiftShift’s plateau detection flags this separately.
  • Compare with consistency — High volume with 1 workout per week means something different than the same volume spread across 4 sessions. Use the calendar heatmap alongside volume charts.

Volume vs. muscle growth

Research suggests that weekly sets per muscle group matter more than raw tonnage for hypertrophy. LiftShift’s muscle heatmap shows weekly sets per muscle group with volume zone scoring (maintenance, growth, or overreaching), so you can cross-reference raw volume with per-muscle weekly set counts for a more complete picture.

Volume zone scoring

LiftShift personalises volume zones based on your training history. The heatmap shows whether each muscle is in:

  • Maintenance — Enough to hold current muscle and strength.
  • Growth — In the range where progressive overload typically drives adaptation.
  • Overreaching — High volume that may exceed recovery capacity over time.

Caveats

Volume is a useful trend metric, not an absolute measure. Bodyweight exercises, partial reps, tempo changes, and technique variations all affect actual training stimulus in ways tonnage can’t capture. Use volume as one signal among many — alongside PR tracking, plateau detection, and set-by-set feedback.
