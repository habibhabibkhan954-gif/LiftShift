1RM (one-rep max)

LiftShift shows 1RM estimates derived from your logged sets so you can track strength trends without testing maxes.

What a 1RM estimate means

A 1RM estimate is a calculated approximation of the maximum weight you could lift for a single repetition, based on a submaximal set (lower weight, more reps). It lets you track strength over time without ever needing to attempt a true one-rep max in the gym.

For example: if you log 80 kg for 8 reps on the bench press, LiftShift estimates your 1RM at roughly 99–101 kg (depending on the formula used). If two months later you log 85 kg for 8 reps, the estimated 1RM rises to around 105–107 kg — a clear strength gain.

How LiftShift calculates 1RM

LiftShift uses industry-standard estimation formulas that account for the non-linear relationship between reps and maximum weight. The estimate becomes more accurate with lower rep counts (1–10 reps) and less accurate at very high reps (15+). This is why the 1RM tracker works best for compound lifts in the strength and hypertrophy rep ranges.

Practical uses for 1RM tracking

  • Track strength trends — When your estimated 1RM rises over weeks, you’re getting stronger, even if you never test a true max.
  • Program percentage-based training — Use your estimated 1RM to calculate working weights (e.g., 75% of 1RM for volume work).
  • Compare exercises — See relative strength across lifts to spot imbalances (e.g., bench 1RM near squat 1RM may indicate overdeveloped pressing or undertrained legs).
  • Detect stalls early — A flat 1RM trend line may reveal a plateau before your rep counts show it.

Limitations

  • Rep range matters — Estimates from sets of 2–3 reps are more accurate than estimates from sets of 12–15 reps, where fatigue and technique breakdown add noise.
  • Technique changes affect comparability — If you switch from a wide grip to a close grip on bench press, the estimate may shift for reasons unrelated to actual strength change.
  • Tempo and range of motion — Slower eccentrics, pauses, or deeper range of motion can lower rep counts at the same weight, which the formula may misinterpret as a strength loss.
  • Not a competition max — An estimate is not the same as actually lifting the weight. Treat it as a trend signal, not a guarantee.

Safety note

If you choose to test a true one-rep max based on the estimate, use proper safety equipment (spotter, safeties, racks) and warm up progressively. LiftShift is an analytics tool, not a coach. Consult a qualified professional before attempting maximal lifts.
